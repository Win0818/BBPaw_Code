American Experts Training Babies to Be “Multi-tasking”

A special study has been cooperatively confirmed by American behaviorists, psychologists and education experts, that children can focus on 2-3 things at the same time, while adults can focus on 4-6 things or even more. Even for a 1-year-old baby just learning to walk, it can take faltering steps while listening to your advice. So it totally can be “Multi-tasking” for a normal person.

Like “Concentration”, its “Distribution” must also be nurtured. It is particularly crucial to consciously cultivate this in infants. Studies have shown that for the babies who can easily complete “multi-tasking” Training, when they need to focus on attention, in turn their abilities have improved, such as lasting persistence, rapid response, body coordination, strong memory, and creativity

American experts suggest babies in different ages should receive multi-task training step by step.

About 1 year old

  • Learning to listen to music and people talking at the same time;

  • Clapping hands while trying to learn to speak;

  • Practicing watching a luminous ball while putting hands up over and over again;

  • Babbling while waving flags;

  • Learning to walk while listening to music.

    • 2-3 years old

  • Learning to sing and step at the same time;

  • Playing football while running;

  • Waving the rhythm of the songs with both hands while singing;

  • Dancing while singing;

  • Doing body movements according to the instructions issued by adults;

  • Making all sorts of facial expressions while telling a simple story;

  • Twisting the body according to the content while reciting nursery rhymes;

  • Holding a doll in one hand, and hugging a teddy bear in other hand;

  • Noticing the numbers being tapped on their back while watching the pictures in front.

    • Above 3 years old

    After receiving “Multi-tasking” Training, babies over 3 tend to perform well in every aspect. With the improvement of mental and physical levels, the training content and difficulty can be gradually increased. Such as:

  • Accurately hitting the complex rhythm of the rumba, tango and other dances while listening to music;

  • Reciting poetry while watching TV;

  • Using the body to stimulate the letter shape while reciting the English alphabet;

  • Playing an instrument while singing.
